# Rani Beauty Clinic — WA State Compliance Research Notes

## 1. WA DOH Licensing Requirements for Medspas

### Business Licenses Required:
- WA State Business License (DOR) — required for all businesses
- "Doing Business As" (DBA) registration if operating under a trade name
- No specific "medspa license" exists — regulated through professional licenses
- Medical director must hold active WA MD license (WMC)
- Facility may need local Renton/King County business permits

### Professional Licenses Required:
- MD: WMC license (RCW 18.71) — Dr. Landfield must maintain active WA license
- RN: WABON license (RCW 18.79)
- LPN: WABON license (RCW 18.79)
- Esthetician: DOL license (RCW 18.16) — 750 hours training
- Master Esthetician: DOL license (RCW 18.16) — 750 + 450 additional hours

### Facility Requirements:
- OSHA compliance (infection control, bloodborne pathogens)
- Medical Test Site (MTS) license from WA DOH if performing blood draws/lab tests
  - Waived MTS license for simple waived tests (glucose, HbA1c, etc.)
  - Application: https://doh.wa.gov/licenses-permits-and-certificates/facilities-z/medical-test-sites-mts/licensing-information
  - WA MTS program is CLIA-exempt — one license covers both MTS and CLIA numbers
- Malpractice insurance (required for all licensed practitioners)
- Workers' compensation insurance (required by WA law for employees)
- General business liability insurance

## 2. Medical Director Requirements (Dr. Landfield)

Source: WMC GUI2020-02 (Medical Directors Guideline, adopted 8/21/20)

### Core Responsibilities:
1. Prioritize staff and patient safety
2. Know and apply practice standards for the specific type of practice
3. Supervise and guide ALL clinical staff (employees AND independent contractors)
4. Ensure each clinical staff member is properly licensed, trained, and acts within their legal scope
5. Coordinate care, promote teamwork and communication
6. Clearly communicate expectations to clinical staff
7. Develop and update policies, guidelines, and protocols for clinical staff
8. Ensure clinical staff exercise independent clinical judgment, put patient first, not influenced by financial interests
9. Respond to emergencies in a timely manner
10. Ensure appropriate medical records are kept; health care information is confidential and secure
11. Promote professionalism and ethical values

### On-Site Requirements (from WAC 246-919-605 and 246-919-606):
- **LLRP Devices (Laser, IPL, RF, Plasma):** Physician must be on-site for INITIAL treatment of each patient
  - Subsequent treatments: physician must be reachable by phone and able to see patient within 60 minutes
  - Absence must be "brief and intermittent" — NOT an ongoing arrangement
  - Back-up physician must agree in writing to cover
- **FDA-Approved Injectables (Botox, fillers):** Physician need NOT be on-site; must be reachable by phone and able to respond within 30 minutes
- **Non-FDA-Approved or Off-Label Substances:** Physician must be ON-SITE for entire duration of procedure
- **No specific minimum hours/days per week** is mandated by statute — but "brief and intermittent" absence language implies regular presence

### Warning from WMC:
"The Commission advises practitioners to be wary of entering into arrangements with unlicensed persons. These relationships may entail legal risks involving aiding or abetting the unlicensed practice of medicine, the corporate practice of medicine, and violating fee-splitting, rebating or anti-kickback laws."

## 4. Delegation Protocols

### For Laser/LLRP Procedures (WAC 246-919-605):
Required written office protocol must include:
- Identity of physician authorized to use device
- Statement of activities, decision criteria, and plan for supervised professional
- Selection criteria to screen patients
- Identification of devices and settings for patients meeting criteria
- Methods for operating and maintaining device
- Description of appropriate care for complications/emergencies
- Documentation method and communication/feedback plan

### For Cosmetic Injectables (WAC 246-919-606):
Required written office protocol must include:
- Identity of physician responsible for delegation
- Selection criteria to screen patients
- Description of appropriate care for complications/emergencies
- Statement of activities, decision criteria, and plan for delegate

### Quality Assurance Program (required for both):
- Mechanism to identify complications and untoward effects
- Mechanism to review adherence to written protocols
- Mechanism to monitor quality of treatments
- Mechanism to incorporate findings into future protocols
- Ongoing training to maintain and improve quality

## 5. Informed Consent Requirements

Source: RCW 7.70.050, RCW 7.70.060; WAC 246-919-606

### General Requirements (RCW 7.70.050):
- Patient must be informed of material facts relating to treatment
- Patient must consent to treatment
- Reasonable patient standard: what a reasonably prudent patient would want to know
- Signed consent form = prima facie evidence of informed consent (RCW 7.70.060)

### Specific Requirements for Cosmetic Procedures (WAC 246-919-606):
- Must list foreseeable side effects and complications
- Must identify the delegate(s) who will perform the procedure (name and license type)
- Patient must sign BEFORE treatment

### Best Practice Elements for Each Procedure:
**Botox/Fillers:**
- Risks: bruising, swelling, asymmetry, ptosis, vascular occlusion, necrosis
- Identity and license of injector
- Off-label use disclosure if applicable
- Photo consent (HIPAA)

**Laser/IPL/RF:**
- Risks: burns, scarring, hyperpigmentation, hypopigmentation, blindness (eye area)
- Disclosure that nonphysician may operate device (WAC 246-919-605(6))
- Identity and license of operator
- Skin type assessment

**GLP-1 Weight Management:**
- Risks: nausea, vomiting, pancreatitis, thyroid C-cell tumor risk
- Off-label use if applicable
- Drug interactions
- Monitoring plan

**Hormone Therapy:**
- Risks specific to therapy type
- Monitoring requirements
- Drug interactions

**IM Vitamin Injections:**
- Risks: injection site reactions, allergic reaction
- Ingredients disclosed

## 6. HIPAA Compliance

### Federal HIPAA Requirements:
- Privacy Rule: protect PHI, provide Notice of Privacy Practices (NPP)
  - **ACTION REQUIRED: Update NPP by February 16, 2026** (new HIPAA Privacy Rule changes)
- Security Rule: administrative, physical, technical safeguards for ePHI
- Breach Notification Rule: notify patients within 60 days of breach discovery
- Business Associate Agreements (BAAs) with all vendors handling PHI

### Washington My Health My Data Act (MHMDA) — Effective March 31, 2024:
Source: RCW 19.373
- Covers BROADER "consumer health data" than HIPAA — includes wellness, cosmetic, online activity, location data
- Applies to ALL Washington clinics, including cash-pay medspas
- Requirements:
  - Publish clear privacy policy on website
  - Obtain opt-in consent before collecting/sharing non-essential data
  - Separate consent for marketing vs. treatment-related data
  - No geofencing within 1,750 feet of healthcare facilities for marketing
  - Retain data sale authorizations for 6 years
  - Establish workflows for patient data access, deletion, consent withdrawal requests
  - Provide list of third parties with whom data has been shared upon request
- Violations: consumers can sue directly under WA Consumer Protection Act (RCW 19.86)

### Practical Compliance Steps:
- Use HIPAA-compliant EHR/practice management software
- Ensure all intake forms, booking platforms, and communication tools are HIPAA-compliant
- Execute BAAs with: EHR vendor, billing software, email/SMS platforms, cloud storage
- Update website privacy policy to address MHMDA
- Audit all marketing pixels, cookies, analytics tools for MHMDA compliance
- Train all staff on HIPAA and MHMDA annually

## 7. Medical Waste Disposal

Source: WA Dept of Ecology (360-407-6000); WA DOH (360-236-4700); RCW 70A.228; Chapter 173-303 WAC

### Categories of Waste at Rani Beauty Clinic:
1. **Sharps waste** (needles from IM injections, Botox, fillers, blood draws): Regulated biomedical waste
2. **Pharmaceutical waste** (expired/unused medications, GLP-1, hormones): Dangerous waste
3. **Laser/treatment waste** (gloves, gauze, treatment materials): May be regulated biomedical waste if blood-contaminated

### Requirements:
- **Sharps containers**: Must be closable, puncture-resistant, leak-proof on sides and bottom; compatible with waste and treatment methods
- **Labeling**: Containers labeled "Hazardous Waste Pharmaceuticals" or "Dangerous Waste Pharmaceuticals"
- **Holding time**: Healthcare facilities may accumulate noncreditable dangerous waste pharmaceuticals on-site for up to 1 year without permit; large quantity generators: 90 days
- **Transportation**: Only companies with valid WA UTC permit can collect, transport, and dispose of biomedical waste
- **Documentation**: Shipping papers and recordkeeping required

### Action Items:
- Contract with a licensed biomedical waste hauler (Stericycle, Daniels Health, BioMedical Waste Solutions)
- Obtain proper sharps containers for each treatment room
- Establish pharmaceutical waste disposal protocol
- Maintain waste disposal manifests/shipping papers

## 9. WA State Advertising Regulations

### Federal Framework (applies in WA):
- **FTC Act (15 U.S.C. § 45)**: Prohibits unfair or deceptive acts in commerce
  - All advertising claims must be truthful, not misleading, and substantiated
  - Before/after photos must show TYPICAL results, not best-case scenarios
  - Must disclose if photos are atypical
  - Testimonials must reflect honest opinions; material connections must be disclosed
- **FDA Regulations**: Cannot advertise prescription drugs directly to consumers in misleading ways
  - Cannot advertise Botox, Dysport, Wegovy, etc. without fair balance of risks
  - Cannot claim FDA-approved drugs treat conditions beyond their approved indications without proper disclosure

### Washington State Framework:
- **RCW 19.86 (Consumer Protection Act)**: Prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices in trade or commerce
  - No materiality required — even technically true but misleading statements can violate CPA
  - Private right of action: consumers can sue for violations
  - AG Ferguson has actively enforced against medical aesthetic businesses (e.g., $5M settlement with Allure Esthetic in 2024 for fake reviews)
- **WMC Rules**: Physician advertising must be truthful and not misleading
  - Cannot guarantee specific results
  - Cannot use misleading before/after photos
  - Cannot make unsubstantiated claims about treatments

### Specific Prohibitions:
- Cannot guarantee specific results ("lose 30 lbs guaranteed")
- Cannot claim treatments "cure" conditions
- Cannot advertise prescription products (GLP-1, hormones) without appropriate disclosures
- Cannot use fake reviews or testimonials
- Cannot use before/after photos without patient written consent (HIPAA)
- Cannot use before/after photos that are not representative of typical results
- Cannot advertise compounded GLP-1 as equivalent to brand-name products
- Cannot use geofencing within 1,750 feet of healthcare facilities (MHMDA)

### Required Disclosures:
- Medical director name and credentials if advertising medical services
- "Results may vary" or similar disclaimer for outcome claims
- Prescription drug risks if advertising prescription services

## 10. Controlled Substance Protocols

### GLP-1 Medications (Semaglutide/Tirzepatide):
- NOT controlled substances (Schedule II-V) — no DEA registration required for GLP-1 alone
- ARE prescription-only medications requiring valid prescriber-patient relationship
- Must be prescribed by licensed prescriber (MD, ARNP, PA)
- Compounding status (as of 2025-2026):
  - Semaglutide shortage declared RESOLVED by FDA
  - Compounded semaglutide no longer permissible as "shortage" exception
  - Only permissible if: patient has specific medical need that commercially available product cannot meet
  - WA Pharmacy Commission: failure to comply with FD&C Act compounding requirements = disciplinary action
  - **ACTION: Rani Beauty Clinic must source FDA-approved brand-name GLP-1 medications (Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro, Zepbound) through licensed pharmacies**

### Testosterone/Hormone Therapy:
- Testosterone: **Schedule III controlled substance** (DEA)
- Dr. Landfield MUST have active DEA registration to prescribe testosterone
- Must check WA PMP before prescribing (WAC 246-470)
- Electronic prescribing required for controlled substances (EPCS) — mandatory in WA since January 1, 2022
- Must use certified EPCS software
- PMP registration: https://doh.wa.gov/public-health-provider-resources/healthcare-professions-and-facilities/prescription-monitoring-program-pmp

### WA Prescription Monitoring Program (PMP):
Source: RCW 70.225; WAC 246-470
- Tracks Schedule II, III, IV, and V dispensing records
- Prescribers must register for PMP access via Secure Access Washington (SAW)
- Must check PMP before prescribing controlled substances (especially opioids and Schedule II-III)
- Multi-state query available for patients who may fill in other states

### Electronic Prescribing (EPCS):
- Required for ALL Schedule II-V controlled substances in WA since January 1, 2022
- Must use certified EPCS software
- Exceptions: technological failure, prescriber outside normal practice location, patient hardship

### Storage Requirements for Controlled Substances:
- Must be stored in a securely locked, substantially constructed cabinet
- DEA Form 222 required for Schedule II ordering
- Biennial inventory required
- DEA Form 41 for destruction of controlled substances

## 11. Patient Record Retention

Source: WMC GUI2017-02; RCW 70.02

### Recommended Retention Periods (WMC Guideline):
- **Adult patients**: 10 years from date of last visit, prescription refill, phone contact, test, or other patient contact
- **Minor patients**: 21 years from date of birth (whichever is longer)
- **Deceased patients**: 6 years from date of death
- **Litigation/problem cases**: Indefinitely
- **Best practice**: Consider indefinite retention if feasible

### Storage Requirements:
- Electronic or paper records must be stored to allow lawful access
- Must maintain confidentiality
- Can contract with third-party custodian (must comply with HIPAA/state confidentiality laws)

### Patient Access Rights (RCW 70.02.080):
- Must provide records to patient within 15 days of request
- Can charge reasonable fee
- Failure to provide records = potential disciplinary action by WMC

### Record Destruction:
- Must be destroyed by secure means (shredding, burning, or other destruction)
- Cannot be placed in public dumpster or recycling bin
- Give patients opportunity to claim records before destruction

### Closing Practice:
- Must notify active patients and patients seen within 3 years
- Notice at least 30 days in advance (90 days best practice)
- Notice by: individual letter + electronic communication + local newspaper
