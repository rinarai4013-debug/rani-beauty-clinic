# Washington State Medspa Regulatory Compliance Audit
**Prepared for:** Rani Beauty Clinic, Renton, WA
**Prepared by:** Manus AI
**Date:** March 17, 2026

This comprehensive compliance audit evaluates Rani Beauty Clinic's operations against current Washington State laws and regulations governing medical spas. The audit covers licensing, medical director oversight, scope of practice, delegation protocols, informed consent, HIPAA, medical waste, equipment certification, advertising, controlled substances, record retention, and recent legislative changes.

## 1. WA DOH Licensing Requirements

Operating a medical spa in Washington State requires a combination of business and professional licenses, as the state does not issue a specific "medspa license." The clinic must maintain a Washington State Business License through the Department of Revenue, along with any required local permits for the City of Renton [1]. 

Because Rani Beauty Clinic offers medical services such as blood work and vitamin injections, the facility must hold a Medical Test Site (MTS) license from the Washington State Department of Health (DOH) [2]. For simple blood draws and basic lab tests, a Waived MTS license is required. Washington's MTS program is exempt from the federal Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments (CLIA), meaning the state-issued MTS license serves as the facility's CLIA certification [2]. Furthermore, all licensed practitioners must maintain active credentials with their respective boards, and the clinic must carry general business liability, professional malpractice, and workers' compensation insurance [3].

## 2. Medical Director Oversight Requirements

As the Medical Director, Dr. Landfield bears ultimate responsibility for the medical care provided and the safety of all patients at Rani Beauty Clinic. The Washington Medical Commission (WMC) requires medical directors to prioritize patient safety, ensure all staff act within their legal scope of practice, and develop written policies and protocols for clinical staff [4].

The physical presence requirements for Dr. Landfield depend on the specific procedures being performed:

| Procedure Type | On-Site Requirement | Regulatory Source |
|----------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| **Laser/IPL/RF Devices** | Must be on-site for the **initial** treatment of each patient. For subsequent treatments, must be reachable by phone and able to see the patient within 60 minutes. | WAC 246-919-605 |
| **FDA-Approved Injectables** | Need not be physically present, but must be reachable by phone and able to respond within 30 minutes to treat complications. | WAC 246-919-606 |
| **Off-Label/Unapproved Substances** | Must be on-site for the **entire duration** of the procedure. | WAC 246-919-606 |

The WMC explicitly states that any absence by the delegating physician during laser treatments must be "brief and intermittent" and cannot be an ongoing arrangement [5]. If Dr. Landfield is temporarily unavailable, a local back-up physician must agree in writing to provide consultation and treat complications [5].

## 3. Scope of Practice by Team Member

Washington State strictly regulates which procedures can be performed by different types of licensed professionals. It is critical that Rani Beauty Clinic ensures no staff member exceeds their legal scope of practice, as doing so could result in disciplinary action against both the staff member and Dr. Landfield [1].

| Role | Cosmetic Injectables (Botox/Fillers) | Laser/Energy Devices | IM Injections & Blood Draws |
|------|--------------------------------------|----------------------|-----------------------------|
| **MD / ARNP** | Yes (Full independent authority) | Yes (Full independent authority) | Yes (Full independent authority) |
| **RN / LPN** | Yes (Under physician delegation) | Yes (Under physician delegation) | Yes (Under physician order) |
| **Master Esthetician** | **NO** (Explicitly prohibited) | Yes (Under physician delegation) | **NO** (Explicitly prohibited) |
| **Basic Esthetician** | **NO** (Explicitly prohibited) | **NO** (OTC devices only) | **NO** (Explicitly prohibited) |

Under no circumstances may an esthetician or master esthetician administer injections of any kind, including Botox, dermal fillers, vitamin B12, or GLP-1 medications [6]. These procedures must be performed by a licensed nurse (RN or LPN) or a physician [7].

## 4. Delegation Protocols

When Dr. Landfield delegates procedures to nursing staff or master estheticians, Washington law requires strict adherence to written office protocols. For laser and energy devices, WAC 246-919-605 mandates that the protocol include patient selection criteria, specific device settings, maintenance methods, and a detailed plan for managing complications [5]. 

Similarly, for nonsurgical cosmetic procedures like Botox and fillers, WAC 246-919-606 requires a written protocol that identifies the delegating physician, outlines decision criteria for the delegate, and establishes a communication plan [8]. The clinic must also implement a formal quality assurance program to monitor treatment quality, review adherence to protocols, and identify any complications [5]. Delegates are prohibited from exercising independent medical judgment when performing these procedures [5].

## 5. Informed Consent Requirements

Washington State law (RCW 7.70.050) requires healthcare providers to obtain informed consent by disclosing all material facts relating to a treatment [9]. A signed consent form serves as prima facie evidence that informed consent was obtained [10].

For cosmetic procedures specifically, WAC 246-919-606 requires that the consent form list all foreseeable side effects and complications [8]. Crucially, the form must explicitly identify the name and license type of the delegate who will perform the procedure [8]. For laser treatments, the patient must be informed that a nonphysician may operate the device [5]. Furthermore, delegates performing cosmetic procedures must wear a name tag that clearly identifies their license type [8].

## 6. HIPAA and Data Privacy Compliance

Rani Beauty Clinic must comply with both federal HIPAA regulations and the stringent Washington My Health My Data Act (MHMDA), which took effect in March 2024 [11]. MHMDA defines "consumer health data" much more broadly than HIPAA, encompassing wellness information, cosmetic treatment preferences, and even website activity [12].

Under MHMDA, the clinic must publish a clear privacy policy on its website and obtain explicit, opt-in consent before collecting or sharing any non-essential data [12]. Digital intake forms must separate consent for medical treatment from consent for marketing communications [12]. The law also strictly prohibits the use of geofencing within 1,750 feet of a healthcare facility for marketing purposes [12]. Because MHMDA allows consumers to sue directly for violations under the Consumer Protection Act, compliance is critical [12]. Additionally, the clinic must update its HIPAA Notice of Privacy Practices by February 16, 2026, to comply with recent federal rule changes [13].

## 7. Medical Waste Disposal

The disposal of medical waste at Rani Beauty Clinic is regulated by the Washington State Department of Ecology and the Department of Health. The clinic generates regulated biomedical waste (sharps from injections and blood draws) and dangerous waste (expired or unused pharmaceuticals, including GLP-1 medications and hormones) [14].

Sharps must be placed in closable, puncture-resistant, and leak-proof containers [15]. Pharmaceutical waste containers must be specifically labeled as "Hazardous Waste Pharmaceuticals" or "Dangerous Waste Pharmaceuticals" [15]. Healthcare facilities may accumulate noncreditable dangerous waste pharmaceuticals on-site for up to one year without a permit [15]. The clinic must contract with a biomedical waste hauling company that holds a valid permit from the Washington Utilities and Transportation Commission (UTC) and must maintain all shipping papers and disposal records [15].

## 8. Equipment Certification and Calibration

While Washington State does not require a specific state-level registration for laser devices, the clinic must verify the FDA classification of all equipment [6]. Master estheticians may only use prescription medical devices under the delegation of a physician [6]. 

Written office protocols must explicitly detail the methods by which each specific device is operated and maintained [5]. The clinic must follow all manufacturer schedules for calibration and maintenance, and document these activities. Furthermore, the clinic must maintain training records demonstrating that all operators are competent in the physics, safety, and application techniques of each specific device [5]. For blood draw equipment, the clinic must adhere to the requirements of its Medical Test Site license, which includes regular proficiency testing [2].

## 9. Advertising Regulations

Advertising for medical aesthetics in Washington is heavily scrutinized by both federal agencies (FTC and FDA) and the state Attorney General under the Consumer Protection Act (RCW 19.86) [16]. The state actively enforces these rules, recently securing a $5 million settlement against a cosmetic surgery practice for manipulating consumer reviews [17].

All advertising claims must be truthful, evidence-based, and not misleading [18]. Before-and-after photographs must represent typical results; if they represent a best-case scenario, this must be clearly disclosed [18]. The clinic cannot guarantee specific outcomes (e.g., "lose 30 lbs guaranteed") or claim that treatments cure conditions [18]. When advertising prescription medications like Botox or GLP-1s, the clinic must provide a fair balance of risks and benefits and cannot promote off-label uses [19]. Patient testimonials must reflect honest opinions, and any material connections must be disclosed [18].

## 10. Controlled Substance Protocols

Dr. Landfield's prescribing of controlled substances, particularly testosterone for hormone therapy, is subject to strict state and federal regulations. Testosterone is a Schedule III controlled substance, requiring Dr. Landfield to maintain an active DEA registration. 

Since January 1, 2022, Washington State has mandated Electronic Prescribing for Controlled Substances (EPCS) for all Schedule II-V drugs [20]. Furthermore, prescribers are legally required to check the Washington Prescription Monitoring Program (PMP) database before prescribing controlled substances to prevent misuse and duplicative prescribing [21]. 

Regarding GLP-1 medications for weight management, the FDA has declared the shortage of semaglutide resolved [22]. Consequently, the Washington Pharmacy Quality Assurance Commission has warned that compounding pharmacies can no longer produce compounded semaglutide under the shortage exception [23]. Rani Beauty Clinic must ensure it is sourcing FDA-approved, brand-name GLP-1 medications (such as Wegovy or Ozempic) through licensed channels, rather than relying on compounded versions [23].

## 11. Patient Record Retention

The Washington Medical Commission provides specific guidelines for the retention of medical records. While there is no single statutory retention period, the WMC recommends that practitioners retain records for adult patients for at least ten years from the date of the last patient contact [24]. For minor patients, records must be kept for 21 years from the date of birth [24].

Records must be stored securely to maintain confidentiality, whether in paper or electronic format [24]. Under RCW 70.02.080, the clinic is legally obligated to make medical records available to a patient within 15 days of a request [24]. When records are eventually destroyed, they must be rendered unreadable through secure means such as shredding or burning; they cannot simply be placed in a recycling bin [24].

## 12. New 2025-2026 Legislation and Updates

Several recent legislative and regulatory updates directly impact Rani Beauty Clinic:

1. **GLP-1 Compounding Restrictions:** As noted above, the resolution of the semaglutide shortage means clinics must transition away from compounded versions of these drugs to avoid enforcement actions by the FDA and state pharmacy boards [22] [23].
2. **Hormone Therapy Coverage (HB 1971):** Effective January 1, 2026, a new Washington law requires most health plans to cover up to a 12-month supply of prescription hormone therapy [25]. This is highly relevant if the clinic intends to bill insurance for these services.
3. **Medical Spa Work Group:** The Washington DOH has convened an interprofessional Medical Spa and Esthetic Services Work Group to clarify regulations around IV hydration, advanced esthetics, cosmetic injectables, and the prescribing of semaglutides [26]. The clinic must monitor the DOH website for new rules emerging from this group.
4. **HIPAA Updates:** The clinic must update its Notice of Privacy Practices by February 16, 2026, to comply with recent federal modifications to the HIPAA Privacy Rule [13].
