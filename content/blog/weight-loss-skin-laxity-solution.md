## Why Weight Loss Causes Loose Skin

Your skin is a living organ, and it adapts to the body it covers. When you carry extra weight for months or years, your skin stretches to accommodate the additional volume. Over time, this stretching breaks down two critical structural proteins:

**Collagen**, which provides your skin with firmness and structure, becomes damaged and less organized when chronically stretched. Think of it as the scaffolding that holds your skin taut. When that scaffolding weakens, skin loses its ability to snap back.

**Elastin**, which gives your skin its ability to stretch and return to its original shape, also degrades over time. Unlike collagen, elastin is very difficult for your body to regenerate naturally once it has been compromised.

When you lose weight, especially at the accelerated pace that GLP-1 medications can produce, the underlying fat volume decreases faster than your skin can contract. The result is excess skin that hangs or sags in areas where fat loss has been most significant.

Several factors influence how severe skin laxity becomes:

- **The amount of weight lost**: Greater total loss increases the likelihood of noticeable laxity
- **The speed of weight loss**: Faster loss gives skin less time to adapt
- **Age**: Collagen and elastin production naturally decline after your mid-twenties
- **Genetics**: Some people simply produce less collagen than others
- **Sun exposure history**: UV damage accelerates the breakdown of structural skin proteins
- **Hydration and nutrition**: Dehydrated, nutrient-depleted skin recovers more slowly
